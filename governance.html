04Governance & control

Autonomy for the systems. Authority for you.

AI that acts on your behalf needs rules worth the name. AIKING designs the control model before the first agent runs — approval paths, access boundaries, evaluation and accountability — so the systems move fast and the decisions stay yours.

Data & security standards
01The position

Governance is not the brake. It is the licence to move.

Most boards hesitate on AI not because the value is unclear — because the control is.

Directors carry the accountability for whatever an autonomous system does in the company's name. When every system has a defined authority, a bounded permission set, a testing regime and a named owner, that accountability becomes manageable — and the question stops being whether you can deploy, and becomes how fast. Governance is what makes speed safe. It is the reason you can deploy at all.

02The control model

Four controls. No exceptions.

Every AIKING system operates inside the same four disciplines — engineered in before deployment, not bolted on after.

01

Approval paths

Each system carries a written line between what it may do alone and what it prepares for sign-off. Consequential actions wait for a human. Everything else moves.

02

Access controls

Permissions bounded by role, data and tool. A system holds exactly the access its job requires — nothing broader, nothing inherited, nothing assumed.

03

Evaluation

Tested against your rules before it touches the business. Monitored against them once it is running. Performance is measured, never taken on trust.

04

Accountability

An audit trail behind every action, an escalation path for every exception, and a named owner for every system. Nothing runs unowned.

03The artefacts

Instruments a board can act on.

Governance that lives in a slide deck governs nothing. These are working documents — written for your business, applied to every system, and kept current as the deployment scales.

  • Authority matrix

    One page that settles what each system may draft, recommend, decide, trigger or escalate — and where human judgement is mandatory.

  • Risk register

    Model, data, vendor, workflow and reputational risks — logged, weighted and assigned to an owner before they become incidents.

  • Evaluation plan

    What good output looks like, how it is tested before launch, how it is monitored in production, and how failures are reviewed and closed.

  • Oversight cadence

    The review rhythm: who reports, what they report, how often — and how the findings feed back into tighter controls.
